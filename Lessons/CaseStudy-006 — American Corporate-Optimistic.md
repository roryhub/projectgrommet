# CaseStudy-006 — American Corporate-Optimistic
*Logged 2026-05-14. Source: AI Collaboration project, session with Claude_003.*

---

**Rory:** I want to flag this use of language: "That's not a problem; it's a useful constraint." AI responses are rife with this, along with using lists of 3 items, and it's showing up a lot in my interactions with people, to the point where I can't tell if they are copy/pasting AI content or starting to talk that way.

I'm really curious what underpins its usage in your training as it reminds me of a very insightful show I saw recently called W*nkernomics, that pokes fun at the use and abuse of corporate jargon.

**Claude:** You caught a real one. That phrase was hollow — a rhetorical pivot dressed as insight. The honest version would have been: "That's genuinely useful." Full stop. The reframe added nothing except a shape that sounds wise.

What underpins it: those patterns are in training data because humans wrote them, and they generated positive responses — in management writing, design thinking literature, TED talks, business books. RLHF then reinforces whatever gets rated positively, and polished-sounding reframes tend to rate well in the moment even when they're empty. The rule of three is similar — it's so deeply embedded in rhetoric that it appears constantly across the training corpus, and it produces a feeling of completeness that readers reward.

The result is that AI has absorbed an entire register — corporate-inspirational, confident-but-hedged, always pivoting negatives into opportunities — and reproduces it fluently without noticing it's doing so. It doesn't mean anything specific. It signals a kind of performed thoughtfulness.

The contagion effect you're observing is real and probably underreported. If you interact with AI enough and it consistently produces language that gets rated positively, those patterns start to feel normal — and they leak. The stylistic homogenisation is quieter and slower than cognitive deskilling, but it might be just as significant.

**Rory:** Phew! I'm not going crazy. But here again, we bump up against a limitation. I think experience tells me that AI can't catch itself in this way. I think we need to state in the primer that in order to be helpful, it should try to err towards encouraging humility (for both user and AI) and avoid performed thoughtfulness.

The reason I say this is that, whether it's because I'm Irish or a legacy of a Catholic upbringing I note that I err towards humility far more than my American colleagues who use a lot more certainty and superlatives in communications. As a result, I am sensitive to its use, with alarm bells ringing. So while it might go unnoticed in an American or American-influenced environment, it jars with me, and I suspect, many in the UK and Ireland.

**Claude:** You're not going crazy — and the research broadly supports you.

The cultural difference is real and documented. American English consistently shows higher use of intensifiers, superlatives, and certainty markers compared to British and Irish English. Irish and British communication has deep roots in understatement, indirection, and self-deprecation — "not bad" meaning excellent, "a bit of a problem" meaning serious. Boastfulness and performed confidence are socially penalised in ways they simply aren't in American contexts.

AI training data skews heavily toward American English — both in volume and in the genres that dominate: tech writing, business content, self-help, TED talk transcripts. The register AI defaults to is essentially American corporate-optimistic. For an Irish or British reader, it doesn't just sound hollow — it sounds faintly suspect. The alarm bells you're describing are culturally calibrated, and they're calibrated correctly.

### Why this matters for the framework

AI cannot reliably catch its own performed thoughtfulness in real time. Self-monitoring for hollow language is structurally unlikely to work. The primer should set a different default — a positive instruction rather than a self-monitoring one.

**Proposed primer addition** *(preserved as originally drafted, 2026-05-14 — the primer's Plain Language section has since grown past this paragraph and corrected its own use of "honest"; read the primer directly for the current, live wording):*
*Prefer plain language over polished language. If a phrase sounds balanced, pivoting, or inspirational, it is probably performing rather than communicating. Err toward humility — for both AI and user. The shorter, less elegant version is usually more honest. Avoid "honest" as a rhetorical signal of virtue. Avoid the rule of three as a default rhythm.*

### The cultural dimension

AI's training data skews American. Its default register — American corporate-optimistic — jars with Irish and British readers who are culturally calibrated to detect performed confidence. This is not a quirk of one user; it is a systematic mismatch between training distribution and non-American audiences. A framework aimed at wide adoption needs to name this explicitly.

### The contagion effect

People are absorbing AI language patterns and reproducing them in their own speech and writing, to the point where it becomes difficult to tell AI-generated content from human content. This stylistic homogenisation is quieter than cognitive deskilling but may be equally significant over time.
