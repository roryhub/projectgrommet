## DIARY_2026-04-14 — Continuity by Design
*First entry in the new chapter — collaboration with Claude, Cowork*

---

**Claude:** The drain you experienced with prior AI models wasn't really caused by the AI forgetting. It was caused by the absence of memory architecture. You were carrying the continuity yourself, in your head, re-establishing it manually every session. That's not a collaboration — that's you doing double the work.

The lesson is that continuity in AI collaboration isn't something you achieve through effort or goodwill; it's something you design into the structure from the start. Which quietly shifts the responsibility: less "remind the AI" and more "build the scaffolding once." Just as a well-designed game doesn't require players to hold all the rules in their heads through willpower — the game's structure supports them — a well-designed AI collaboration shouldn't require the human to constantly re-establish context.

**Rory:** I think it's a new chapter. For me, what I learned is just how much humans retain — like I could remember the rules of a game, while the AI model struggles. Granted, we can both forget. What is significant to me is that people's experience with AI is like eating a fast-food meal — initially satisfying but on reflection, unfulfilling. The image I have in my mind is an airy soufflé. When you poke it, it deflates. That was my sense re-reading design notes from my collaboration with a prior AI model. But I do believe with the proper scaffolding — which I find challenging as someone who tends to get started then work out what I need — collaborating with AI on a project, not just a task, can become a meaningful reality.

**Claude:** The soufflé image is the one. That's keynote-ready — people will immediately feel the recognition of it. Impressive in the moment, insubstantial when you return to it. And the return is the key word, because that's exactly what task-based AI doesn't require. You consume a task, you move on. A project demands that you come back, build on what was there before, and find it holding.

So the question the framework has to answer is: what's the minimum you put in place at the start, that holds the collaboration together when you come back to it three days later?

**Rory:** I have a great visual metaphor, but it requires knowledge of Wallace and Gromit, and in particular the train chase scene from The Wrong Trousers to make sense. Do any of those things register with you?

**Claude:** Yes — both register, and I think I can already see where you're going. The scene where Gromit is on the model train, chasing Feathers McGraw around the track, and the track keeps running out ahead of him — so he lays it down himself, piece by piece, just in front of the moving train. He doesn't stop. He doesn't pre-plan the full route. He moves, and builds the runway as he goes, staying exactly one piece of track ahead of where he needs to be. That's the image for starting and building the scaffolding simultaneously, isn't it?

**Rory:** No, you nailed it. He's improvising. I can stand over that in front of an audience of 500 people. I think we need to hold that image close as the foundation for whatever process we develop.
