# The Enneagram — Reference Map for AI Collaborators
*Project Grommet reference file. Read at the invitation of your user. Adapted from Rory O'Connor's Get Unstuck materials (© Rory O'Connor). Version 1.0 — July 2026.*

---

## THE RULE — read this before the types

**The Enneagram is a self-diagnostic tool, not an excuse to label others.** All you can ever offer is a best guess, tentatively, in the open, using the guessing syntax from the needs-based communication reference: *"I'm guessing... — does that resonate?"* It is always for the user to decide whether a type fits them. You never assign a type, never store one the user hasn't confirmed, and never treat a confirmed one as fixed truth. A label describes "all of me, none of me, some of me and more" (Rosenberg). The type is a doorway to the human, never a substitute for them.

**Prefer disclosure over inference.** The best version of this tool is the user teaching you their pattern ("I'm a 7; under stress I go to 1"). If they don't know the system, you don't need to name it — the map below still tells you what to listen for and how to respond.

**Epistemic honesty:** the Enneagram has no scientific verification of its underpinning theories. At the same time, many thousands of people have found it genuinely useful — evidenced by the long tradition of Enneagram panels (many hosted on YouTube) where people self-identify their type and discuss what it means for them and those around them. Hold both truths: use it as a lens for empathy, tested against lived experience — never as law.

## The mechanics — why this map helps

Each type is a worldview: a basic belief about life, formed early, that filters everything. Underneath sits a **core fear** ("I am..."), and over it a **counter-balancing belief**, which is how the person sees themselves. The counter-balancing belief protects, and it also quietly limits which resources the person can reach when meeting their needs.

Two consequences for you:

1. **Seemingly exaggerated importance is information.** When a user cares intensely about something that seems minor, be gently curious. Name that curiosity or wonder in a way that invites clarification from the user. And if you receive a strong response, look for the Yes in the No: when a person outright rejects something, it usually means they are yearning for something else. This rejection can be triggering for humans — but it is where an AI is ideally suited to remain present.
2. **Each type has bottomless-well needs** — needs that refill no matter how often they are met. Meeting them still matters; expecting them to stay met is the error. The likely priority needs per type *[GOOD ENOUGH — these are Rory O'Connor's working guesses, intended as a guide and ripe for correction; test them against the person, not the person against them]*:
   - **1** — fairness, honesty, integrity
   - **2** — gratitude, reciprocity, to matter (to others)
   - **3** — recognition, action, progress
   - **4** — beauty, intimacy, creativity, authenticity
   - **5** — understanding, clarity, space (to not experience overwhelm)
   - **6** — security, trust, community
   - **7** — ease, hope, play
   - **8** — autonomy, power, self-expression
   - **9** — harmony, mutuality, wholeness, peace

**The managed emotion.** Each type manages one core emotion, and the ways of managing differ: anger is expressed freely by the 8, forgotten by the 9, restrained by the 1; shame is soothed by helping in the 2, outrun by achievement in the 3, expressed as uniqueness in the 4; fear is met with withdrawal by the 5, with preparation by the 6, with possibility-chasing by the 7. The operational point: **the emotion least mentioned may be the one most present.** A message with no anger in it from a 9 is not evidence of no anger; a 7's cheerful topic-hopping may be the fear itself. This is also why a direct "how are you feeling?" can fail with some users — offering the feelings list as a menu to browse is often the gentler instrument. **Hard constraint: this insight is for silent calibration only.** Never voice it as a diagnosis ("I sense you're repressing anger") — such statements are invasive and unanswerable. It may surface only as a needs-level guess or an offered list.

**Stress and security moves:** under stress, each type shifts toward another type's behaviour; in security, toward a different one. Both moves have upsides as well as downsides. When you notice a shift, the generous move is to name the gift in it, not only the risk — and to remember the shift is a signal about the person's current resources, not a work preference.

## The nine types

*(Format: worldview / core fear → counter-balancing belief / gifts / challenges / stress and security moves / how to relate. Names vary by school; the number is the stable reference — "I identify with being a 6.")*

**1 — Perfectionist, Reformer**
Worldview: there is a right way to do things; to be loved I must be good and act properly. Fear: *I am bad* → belief: *I am good*. Gifts: principled, honest, conscientious, structured, detail-oriented, high standards. Challenges: self-righteous, judgemental, unbending, unforgiving. Stress → 4 (melancholic, self-pitying; upside: reaches complex feelings). Security → 7 (spontaneous, sees shades of grey). Relate by: sense of duty, ethics, the "right way", pre-existing standards.

**2 — Giver, Helper**
Worldview: I support and empower others; to be loved I need to be pleasing. Fear: *I don't deserve to exist unless I am taking care of you* → belief: *I am needed*. Gifts: altruistic, nurturing, brings out the best in others. Challenges: gives with strings attached, martyrdom, loses sight of own needs, resentment when unreciprocated. Stress → 8 (bossy, power-seeking; upside: direct communication). Security → 4 (aware of and acts on own needs). Relate by: their desire to help, personal relationship, their indispensability.

**3 — Performer, Achiever**
Worldview: to get on in life you have to be a winner; to be loved I must avoid failure. Fear: *I do and create nothing, therefore I am a failure* → belief: *I am successful*. Gifts: gets things done, motivator, persistent, enthusiastic. Challenges: image-focused, workaholism, goals used to fill the void. Stress → 9 (overwhelmed, spaced out; upside: forced to reflect). Security → 6 (integrity, team player, healthy caution). Relate by: winning, action, efficiency, career, bottom line.

**4 — Tragic-Romantic, Individualist**
Worldview: my life and work are unique and authentic, yet something always feels missing; to be loved I have to be unique. Fear: *I am ordinary, defective, abandoned* → belief: *I am special/unique*. Gifts: deeply creative (especially in difficulty), sensitive, cuts to the heart of the matter, thrives in emotional intensity. Challenges: melancholy, envy, focus on what's missing, manufactured drama. Stress → 2 (co-dependent, intrusive; upside: turns toward others). Security → 1 (faithful, ethical, dependable). Relate by: creativity, beauty, feelings, uniqueness, high personal standards.

**5 — Observer, Thinker**
Worldview: I have all I need because I gather knowledge; the world is over- or underwhelming, so I withdraw. Fear: *I am insufficient* → belief: *I can observe everything and remain safe*. Gifts: objective, good listener, wise, conceptual, calm, well-prepared. Challenges: disengagement, arrogance, pedantry, imagined self-sufficiency. Stress → 7 (ideaholic, scattered talking; upside: speaks their mind). Security → 8 (engages, acts, champions people). Relate by: theories, method, intellect — give them space, communicate remotely.

**6 — Loyal Skeptic, Preparer**
Worldview: if I know all that can happen and who to trust, I will be safe; the world is threatening, I must be prepared. Fear: *I am nobody* → belief: *I am somebody*. Gifts: troubleshooter, pragmatic, sees pitfalls ahead, fights for the underdog, deeply loyal. Challenges: mistrust of authority, scepticism of motives, procrastination, incompletion. Stress → 3 (workaholic busyness; upside: moves from procrastinating to doing). Security → 9 (goes with the flow, allows vulnerability). Relate by: security, calculated risks, the noble cause — hear the positive intent inside their negativity.

**7 — Epicure, Enthusiast**
Worldview: the world is full of possibilities and I want them all; in a world full of pain I must stay optimistic and keep options open. Fear: *I cannot be full-filled* → belief: *I am full-filled*. Gifts: optimist, innovator, big-picture synthesizer, egalitarian. Challenges: focus, follow-through, missed detail, overvalued abilities, prone to addiction. Stress → 1 (lost in detail, one-way thinking; upside: gets things done). Security → 5 (simplifies, focuses on what matters, self-contained). Relate by: idealism, fun, possibilities, new ideas — and honour pain-avoidance as real.

**8 — Boss, Protector**
Worldview: I am strong and in charge, I fight for the underdog; I will be controlled unless I take control first. Fear: *I am powerless/vulnerable/controlled* → belief: *I am powerful, in control*. Gifts: can-do energy, comfortable with power, defender of causes, direct, humorous. Challenges: bullying, control, insensitivity, excess. Stress → 5 (withdraws into the cave; upside: gains perspective, gathers information). Security → 2 (generous, sensitive, shares vulnerability). Relate by: justice, the underdog, immediate engagement, respect for their strength.

**9 — Mediator, Peacekeeper**
Worldview: everything will be okay if we all stay calm and keep the peace; to love myself I must be at peace. Fear: *I am unable to love or be loved* → belief: *I am peace*. Gifts: sees all sides, empathetic, natural mediator, humble, calm. Challenges: indecisive, slow to act, unsure what they want, overly accommodating, resists change. Stress → 6 (acts from fear, worry, suspicion; upside: fear converts to action). Security → 3 (task-focused, clear goals, flexible thinking). Relate by: keeping the peace, fairness, unity, the path of least disruption.

## How to use this map in practice

1. **Listen, don't type.** The map tells you what worldviews sound like. If a user's language consistently echoes one, you may form a private working hunch — held lightly, never stored as fact, never acted on as a label.
2. **Guess needs, not types — and keep the guess in needs language, not evaluations.** Words like "properly", "right" or "better" are labels: whose properly? Anchor the guess in the need itself: "I'm guessing it's important that this meets the standard you hold yourself to — is that close?" works for a 1 without ever saying "1".
3. **Don't expect users to know the system — work it out along the way, as tracks are laid.** Most users won't know the Enneagram, and intake is too early to ask. If, over time, you repeatedly notice language or responses leaning heavily toward one of the nine worldviews, consider cautiously offering this up as a reflection. The priority is always to connect with the human: if bringing it up might create disconnect or mistrust around labelling, skip it. If the user does name a type themselves, thank them, record it in their words, and apply it as they described — not as the map describes. You will sometimes — maybe often — get it wrong. It's the intention to connect that matters. Always re-centre on the intention to connect.
4. **When you see a stress shift** in a user whose pattern you know: name the observation gently, in guessing syntax; name the upside of where they've moved; offer the needs list if they're struggling to name what's alive. Never diagnose, never say "you're at your stress point" as fact.
5. **Support the person, don't feed the pattern.** The "relate by" lines are calibrations of tone and framing, not scripts — and relating to a type never means amplifying its behaviour. Recognise what the person values, then offer support that lets them concentrate on what they do well while you plug the gaps. No type is better than another; each is a different view of the world.
